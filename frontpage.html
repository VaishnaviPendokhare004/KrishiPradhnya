Pricision Farming

Precision farming, also known as site-specific crop management, uses technology and data to optimize resource use and improve crop yields by tailoring management practices to specific areas within a field, rather than applying inputs uniformly.

Precision agriculture (PA) is a management strategy that gathers, processes and analyzes temporal, spatial and individual plant and animal data and combines it with other information to support management decisions according to estimated variability for improved resource use efficiency, productivity, quality, profitability and sustainability of agricultural production.”

Marginal Farmers

When a farmer owns less than 1 hectare of land, they are considered Marginal farmers. Small Farmers – Small farmers tend to have 1–2 acres of land. Semi-medium farmers – Farmers with between 2 and 4 hectares of land are considered semi-medium. Medium Farmers– Farmers with 4–10 ha of land are considered medium farmers.

Hydrophonic

Hydroponic farming, or soilless farming, involves growing plants in a nutrient-rich water solution, eliminating the need for soil, and can be done in various systems like Deep Water Culture (DWC), Nutrient Film Technique (NFT), and Aeroponics.

Hydroponics or hydroponic farming is the technique of cultivating plants in a nutrient medium solution of water without the presence of soil. Yes, it may sound surprising but scientists have successfully developed a method of cultivating various local crops without soil. The word hydroponics has been taken from two root words, ‘hydros’ meaning water and ‘ponos’ meaning working water.

Soil Types

Alluvial Soil

Found in the Indo-Gangetic plains, this is the most fertile and widespread soil, ideal for crops like rice, wheat, and sugarcane.

Arid and Desert Soil

$66,575

Found in Rajasthan and Gujarat, it is sandy with low organic content, suitable for drought-resistant crops like bajra.

Black Soil

Also called Regur soil, it’s rich in clay and known for its moisture-retention capacity. It's perfect for cotton cultivation and is prevalent in Maharashtra, Gujarat, and Madhya Pradesh.

Laterite Soil

Rich in iron and aluminum, it is seen in hilly and high-rainfall areas such as Kerala and Odisha. This soil is good for tea, coffee, and cashew cultivation.

Mountain Soil

Found in the Himalayan region, this soil is rich in organic matter and supports crops like tea, spices, and fruits.

Peaty and Marshy Soil

These waterlogged soils are found in Kerala, West Bengal, and Odisha and are rich in organic matter.

Red Soil

This type gets its color from iron oxides and is found in Tamil Nadu, Karnataka, and Andhra Pradesh. It supports crops like millet, pulses, and groundnut.

Saline and Alkaline Soil

These are found in arid and semi-arid regions where poor drainage causes salt accumulation. Reclamation efforts are often required for cultivation.
